In recent months (and years) I have written about hitting those predictable institutional thresholds — the natural cycles where a senior technologist begins to look at the horizon and ask what comes next. I’ve joked openly about [midlife crises](/blog/another-midlife-crisis), analyzed the predictable patterns of the [seven-year itch](/blog/seven-year-itch), and questioned what it means to be [a "veteran"](/blog/am-I-old-now) in an industry that routinely worships youth.

Now, I’m looking at a very specific set of numbers.

<!-- truncate -->

I am 50 years old. Nearly 51, in fact, which puts me almost exactly two-thirds of the way through my expected working life.

Having started at 21 straight out of university, I have somewhere between 7 and 17 years remaining until I can draw down my personal and UK state pensions and commit to a life of leisure for however many years remain on the clock.

The recent loss of my father-in-law has brought our family face-to-face with the reality of life's transitions, and has rebalanced the equation somewhat — the loss of a genuinely lovely man who welcomed me into his family on the one hand, but his legacy could also meaningfully alter our financial landscape, with the potential to become mortgage free.

When you remove such a major economic pressure from a career equation, particularly at an age where your personal priorities are already shifting, the metrics change significantly. Success is no longer measured by corporate grandstanding, chasing fancy executive titles — which I have always despised — or building empires of headcount.

It's measured by pride in your work, quality of life, work-life balance, and cognitive alignment.

## The management fork in the road

Somewhere around 20 years ago, I arrived at the fork in the road which faces most technologists as they become more skilled and experienced - become a manager, or remain an individual contributor?

I resisted the management path for a while, prefering to stay "hands on", but as some point the transition became either inevitable, irresisitible, or impractical (or all of the above), and for the last 15+ years I have operated in leadership and executive roles — delivering global projects, managing large engineering footprints, driving product architecture, and scaling pyramidial team structures.

The standard corporate handbook says that once you reach this level, you stay there. You spend your days navigating shifting political priorities, continuous context-switching, and operational firefighting.

But over years of leading, I have learned how my own brain operates. I am wired for deep focus, systemic pattern recognition, and uncompromised domain mastery. I am at my absolute best when I am given a complex problem, insulated from distraction, and allowed to systematically unpack it. Conversely, a chaotic environment of unclear priorities and endless executive context-switching is a direct path to cognitive friction and executive overload.

That came home in rather a profound way recently when I had to take some time off work to deal with stress and support my wife in her grief. The stress part is something that many people would probably resist admitting, but as I took time out to reflect and to wrap up my recent series on the micro- and macro-level impacts of AI, I realised that the modern engineering organization desperately needs something different than traditional pyramid managers.

As AI compresses raw execution and [hollows out junior pipelines](/blog/ai-engineering-team-structure#the-pipeline-problem-doesnt-go-away), [teams are fracturing into smaller, diamond-shaped structures](/blog/ai-engineering-team-structure). Value has migrated entirely away from production speed and toward [high-context engineering judgement](/blog/ai-judgement-economy).

In the burgeoning age of AI, organizations don't just need people to manage the noise; they need deeply-focussed technical anchors who have the multi-decade experience that builds judgement, scar tissue from having been at the sharp end and survived, and the capability to look at a complex system and prevent fires before they ever start.

## The next horizon

I am giving very serious consideration to treating my two-thirds horizon as a deliberate, structured transition away from the management track and back into a senior Individual Contributor footprint — probably in a Staff or Principal Architect role, but I am more motivated by the challenge than the specific label.

Because some of my key financial baselines are soon likely to be covered, I have an opportunity to filter for environment over compensation. I can look to partner with mature, stable organizations, research labs, or sustainable engineering teams that have deeply complex, well-defined technical puzzles but lack the senior architectural oversight to solve and evolve them.

In that ideal world I would be explicitly looking for an environment that respects:

1. **Domain knowledge over Headcount:** I want to own architectural outcomes, technology stack governance, and systemic resilience — not multiple line management or budget politics.
2. **Deep Focus over Firefighting:** I thrive where priorities are transparent and where success is measured by the strategic execution rather than frantic, daily, tactical pivoting.
3. **Genuine Work-Life Balance:** I am looking for something to get my teeth into and to deliver strong engineering judgment during working hours, with the autonomy and stability to disconnect when the day is done.

I've spent 25 years building the knowledge, judgement, and structural intuition required to guide platforms through massive technological shifts. Now, I think I want to deploy that intuition in its purest, most focused form.

If your engineering organisation has an architectural bottleneck, an AI integration strategy that needs sober systemic evaluation, or core platforms that require veteran technical stewardship without the overhead of management noise, let's skip the recruiters. Drop me a DM, and let’s grab a quiet coffee (decaf for me, I'm afraid).
